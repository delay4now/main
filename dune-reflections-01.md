# Reflections on Dune

I was on a flight to Dubai (on my way to the US) when I first watched Dune: Part One, and I was blown away by its fictional world that had many parallels with the real world. My mother had passed away a few months earlier, and even though I’m not religious myself, I had performed the after-death rituals as per her wish, and those were still fresh in my mind. For every living being, death is a defining moment in what remains after one has lived. Disagreements over after-death rituals (e.g., cremation, burial, the Tower of Silence) and legal or moral views around death (e.g., euthanasia, non-violence, fast unto death, suicide) have always intrigued me.

Traditions that evolve over generations often convey meanings that are difficult to understand without personal effort or lived experience. This applies as much to scientific or academic traditions as it does to religious or cultural ones. Just a few centuries ago, we did not have universal acceptance of ideas that now seem obvious in retrospect (e.g., the decimal system, the earth going around the sun). What is instantly obvious to one person need not be obvious to another.

Many conflicts today are born out of misunderstanding or dismissing another person's lived experience through hasty reasoning, without personal effort or reflection. Dune had many such examples. It made me think about the evolution of death rituals (e.g., burial in Islam, immersion of ashes in Hinduism) from a geographical viewpoint for the first time in my life. The evolution of young Paul Atreides into the messiah Muad'Dib also had many moments about how a person grapples with the boundary between confidence and self-doubt in the face of destiny (or chance events).

There is a lot more to say about Parts One and Two, but this is good enough to start a conversation, I believe. I haven't read the trilogy (or the subsequent books) yet, although I have unfortunately read many spoilers on the internet.
