# Reflections on the Movie *The Disciple*

[*The Disciple*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Disciple_(2020_film)) (2020) is a critically acclaimed Marathi language film. It explores the artistic and spiritual journey of Sharad, training to be a classical vocalist. Sharad’s sincerity and idealism conflict with the harsh realities (and spiritual degeneration) of the contemporary, commercial era, bringing forth several deeper themes.

## Devotion to & the Pursuit of the Absolute

### Maai Tapes

These are the recordings of Maai’s voice, an accomplished vocalist herself (Maai means mother or grandmother in Marathi). Sharad keeps listening to these tapes for inspiration. Even though he lacks the talent to become a star vocalist, his efforts and devotion are sincere, and he listens to these tapes as if they were discourses from sacred scriptures by an otherworldly master. He is often listening to the tapes in a meditative manner while riding his motorcycle late at night, on the quiet, asleep streets of Bombay. The serene and poignant voice of Maai would give a Marathi viewer goosebumps!

### Remark on the Tapes

[Guru-Shishya Parampara (Teacher-Disciple Tradition)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guru%E2%80%93shishya_tradition): Sharad lives a life of hardship and sacrifice, following the teachings of his Guru and the recorded lectures of Maai. His journey is as much about achieving excellence in music as is about achieving the spiritual and existential purity, as demanded by the Guru and Maai.

The dharma of the Shishya (Disciple) is to trust and surrender to his Guru (Teacher) in pursuit of the Absolute. The dharma of the Guru is to make himself worthy of the trust. Guru’s greatest joy is for the Disciple to surpass him. Their relationship is revered as sacred and the tradition is steeped into the Indian culture (in spite of the challenges and harsh realities of the contemporary era). Glimpses of the tradition appear in the show [Kurukshetra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurukshetra:_The_Great_War_of_Mahabharata), wherein the Kauravas and the Pandavas are the Shishyas of the Guru Dronacharya. A caricaturized (and insensitive) take appears in the Pai Mei’s training of the Bride in [Kill Bill - Vol 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kill_Bill:_Volume_2).

## Internal and External Struggles

Despite his sincerity of efforts, Sharad is struggling at different fronts. Internally he goes through self-doubt and frustration after a series of failures. Externally the contemporary world is taking every opportunity to mock him and his idealistic pursuit. In a soul-crushing scene, a mediocre individual in the music industry makes derogatory remarks about Maai, the very ideal that Sharad holds as sacred. An amateur achieving overnight success on a flashy talent show reflects the degradation of “true art” into a commercial and fleeting form – a common sight in the contemporary world.

Sharad’s meditative motorcycle ride contrasts with the busy and chaotic life in modern Bombay. These are the few moments he has a tantalizing glimpse of the truth. The subtle off-center framing symbolizes that the hidden truth remains just out of his reach, perhaps because he is not yet “centered”, the root-cause of all his struggles.

In the final shot, Sharad watches a street musician (a beggar) on a local train who plays for nothing but his "daily bread". The beggar becomes the film's final symbol of "selfless artistic truth"—perhaps the only one remaining who truly lives the art without the ego.

## The Essence of Art (and All Activity)

I interpret the voice of the otherworldly master Maai as the symbol of seemingly unattainable “Absolute”. Her lectures equate music with a "Path to the Divine" that requires total dedication and renunciation. The constant hum of the Tanpura (a string instrument) represents the "Eternal Quest". The hum often appears on the soundtrack even when no instrument is visible, as if arising from Sharad’s own soul. It symbolizes the “frequency of truth” that the artist must align with before he can even begin his rehearsal.

A common message across many cultures, as seen by examples below, is: every activity, whether it be music, mathematics, pottery, carpentry, or even eating, swordmaking, and butchery(!), ought to “have a spiritual dimension”, and be “in the service of God”, “in glorification of the Divine”.

### Carpentry
> A story about a carpenter going about his work in a “spiritual manner” attributed to [Chuang Tzu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhuang_Zhou) is as follows: Ch’ing, the chief carpenter, was carving wood into a stand for musical instruments. When finished, the work appeared to those who saw it as though of supernatural execution; and the Prince of Lu asked him, saying, ‘What mystery is there in your art?’

> ‘No mystery, Your Highness,’ replied Ch’ing. ‘And yet there is something. When I am about to make such a stand, I guard against any diminution of my vital power. I first reduce my mind to absolute quiescence. Three days in this condition, and I become oblivious of any reward to be gained. Five days, and I become oblivious of any fame to be acquired. Seven days, and I become unconscious of my four limbs and my physical frame. Then, with no thought of the Court present in my mind, my skill becomes concentrated, and all disturbing elements from without are gone. I enter some mountain forest, I search for a suitable tree. It contains the form required, which is afterwards elaborated. I see the stand in my mind’s eye, and then set to work. Beyond that there is nothing. I bring my own native capacity into relation with that of the wood. What was suspected to be of supernatural execution in my work was due solely to this.’

### Meat-cutting
> Another story, this one about a butcher! This again attributed to [Chuang Tzu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhuang_Zhou) is as follows: Prince Hui’s cook was cutting up a bullock. Every blow of his knife, every heave of his shoulders, every tread of his foot, every whshh of rent flesh, every chhk of the chopper, was in perfect harmony—rhythmical like the Dance of the Mulberry Grove, simultaneous like the chords of the Ching Shou.
> 
> ‘Well done!’ cried the Prince. ‘Yours is skill indeed.’
> 
> ‘Sire,’ replied the cook, ‘I have always devoted myself to Tao. It is better than skill. When I first began to cut up bullocks, I saw before me simply whole bullocks. After three years’ practice I saw no more whole animals. And now I work with my mind and not with my eye. When my senses bid me stop, but my mind urges me on, I fall back upon eternal principles. I follow such openings or cavities as there may be, according to the natural constitution of the animal. I do not attempt to cut through joints, still less through large bones.
> 
> ‘A good cook changes his chopper once a year—because he cuts. An ordinary cook, once a month—because he hacks. But I have had this chopper nineteen years, and though I have cut up many thousands of bullocks, its edge is as if fresh from the whetstone. For at the joints there are always interstices, and the edge of a chopper being without thickness, it remains only to insert that which is without thickness into such an interstice. By these means the interstice will be enlarged, and the blade will find plenty of room. It is thus that I have kept my chopper for nineteen years, as though fresh from the whetstone.
> 
> ‘Nevertheless, when I come upon a hard part, where the blade meets with a difficulty, I am all caution. I fix my eyes on it. I stay my hand, and gently apply the blade, until with a hwah the part yields like earth crumbling to the ground. Then I withdraw the blade and stand up and look around; and at last I wipe my chopper and put it carefully away.’
> 
> ‘Bravo!’ cried the Prince. ‘From the words of this cook I have learnt how to take care of my life.’

## The Relevance, Here and Now

It is March 2026. On one hand, a catastrophic war is going on in the West Asia that could escalate even further into a global nuclear armageddon. On the other hand, the tech titans are obsessed with building an AI workforce to replace the pesky humans. There is breathlessness in the air and making predictions beyond a few months seems foolhardy.

In such times, ideas from traditional cultures can help center the mind and focus on what is truly important. It is worth contemplating about the basic question: What gives meaning to our day-to-day life? Why do we work, produce art, or for that matter perform any activity? For money, status, happiness? What is happiness? In traditional cultures, the answer, admittedly in a very idealized form, is that every activity is (or ought to be) aimed at bringing oneself ever closer to the Divine. Could this change of perspective be an antidote to the self-imposed necessity of self-destruction of the contemporary world?
